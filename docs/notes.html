PhD Qualifying Exam – Study Notes

How I Studied

I’d like to share how I studied for my PhD qualifying exam, in case it helps others.

My most important realization was this: learn derivation, not memorization. A superficial understanding is not enough. In the exam, what matters is not remembering the result, but being able to derive how that result is reached.

For example, unless you can derive the relationship from utility to risk premium and then to ARA, you have not truly learned the concept.

Therefore, I studied each topic at three levels:

  • intuition (why it is correct)
  • mathematical derivation (how it is derived)
  • economic meaning (what it implies)

Without the combination of these three, knowledge is not truly internalized.

Textbooks teach, but they do not teach exam logic. That is why past exam questions are extremely valuable. They show you how to think from the examiner’s perspective.

I never trusted the feeling of “I understood.” My test was simple: close everything and derive the result from scratch. If I couldn’t, I hadn’t learned it.

The biggest mistake is passive studying — reading, highlighting, etc. Real learning comes from writing, deriving, and solving.

Learning ≠ solving.

In a PhD qualifying exam, what matters is not just knowing the material, but being able to build it from scratch and connect concepts across fields.

The best test of understanding is explaining it clearly to someone else. If you cannot explain it simply, you do not fully understand it.

This process taught me one thing: what makes the difference is not working hard, but working correctly.

Banking

Banks are not institutions — they are a collection of contracts.

Key question: What problem exists? Which contract solves it?

  • Markets fail due to adverse selection and moral hazard.
  • Diamond–Dybvig: liquidity provision and bank-run risk stem from the same contract.
  • Loan contracts involve screening, monitoring, and incentive design.
  • Risk is redistributed within contracts.
  • Securitization and shadow banking involve repackaging risk.

Learning banking is learning contract theory. Everything else is detail.

Corporate Finance

Corporate finance is not about memorizing decisions, but about understanding incentive design.

Financial decisions such as capital structure, dividends, and investment are made under information asymmetry and agency problems.

Key question: What is the problem, and what mechanism solves it?

  • Modigliani–Miller is a benchmark: without frictions, financial structure is irrelevant.
  • Capital structure becomes meaningful once taxes, bankruptcy costs, agency problems, and asymmetric information enter the picture.
  • The firm can be viewed as a collection of contracts.
  • Debt is not just financing; it is also a disciplinary mechanism.
  • With asymmetric information, financing choices become signals.

Corporate finance is not about decisions alone — it is about the information and incentives behind them.

Econometrics

Econometrics is not about techniques, but about understanding estimators.

I studied econometrics both empirically and theoretically, but focused especially on the theoretical side. Without theory, empirical work becomes mechanical.

Key question: Why does this estimator look like this?

  • β = true parameter
  • β̂ = estimator
  • β̃ = alternative estimator under different assumptions

OLS is not a formula — it is a solution to an optimization problem.

  • minimization problem
  • normal equations
  • projection logic
  • assumptions behind estimator properties

Gauss–Markov is not a result to memorize, but a logic: under which assumptions is β̂ BLUE, and what does “best” mean?

Heteroskedasticity and serial correlation are not just “problems” — they change the distribution of β̂.

The goal is not just to know tests, but to understand estimator behavior.

Asset Pricing

Asset pricing is about how risk is priced.

I studied CAPM, mean-variance analysis, and APT as different expressions of the same problem: which risks are priced, and why?

  • Mean-variance analysis is the foundation.
  • CAPM is an equilibrium result, not just a formula.
  • Only systematic risk is priced.
  • β measures exposure to market risk.
  • APT generalizes CAPM to multiple priced risk factors.

Asset pricing is not about formulas — it is about understanding the risk–return relationship.

Behavioral Finance

Behavioral finance is not just about irrationality — it is about systematic deviations and their effect on prices.

  • Efficient markets and limits to arbitrage form the key framework.
  • Irrationality alone is not enough; arbitrage must be limited for mispricing to persist.
  • Prospect theory, overconfidence, representativeness, and availability help explain investor behavior.
  • The key chain is: bias → behavior → market outcome.
  • Sentiment, attention, and emotion also shape prices.

Behavioral finance is not a list of biases — it is a framework linking behavior to prices.
