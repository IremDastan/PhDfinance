PhD Qualifying Exam – Study Notes

How I Studied

I’d like to share how I studied for my PhD qualifying exam, in case it helps others.

My most important realization was this: learn derivation, not memorization. A superficial understanding is not enough. In the exam, what matters is not remembering the result, but being able to derive how that result is reached.

For example, unless you can derive the relationship from utility to risk premium and then to ARA, you have not truly learned the concept.

Therefore, I studied each topic at three levels:

  • intuition (why it is correct)
  • mathematical derivation (how it is derived)
  • economic meaning (what it implies)

Without the combination of these three, knowledge is not truly internalized.

Textbooks teach, but they do not teach exam logic. That is why past exam questions are extremely valuable — they show you how to think from the examiner’s perspective.

I never trusted the feeling of “I understood.” My test was simple: close everything and derive the result from scratch. If I couldn’t, I hadn’t learned it.

The biggest mistake is passive studying — reading, highlighting, etc. Real learning comes from writing, deriving, and solving.

Learning ≠ solving.

In a PhD qualifying exam, what matters is not just knowing the material, but being able to build it from scratch and connect concepts across fields.

The best test of understanding is explaining it clearly to someone else. If you cannot explain it simply, you do not fully understand it.

This process taught me one thing: what makes the difference is not working hard, but working correctly.

Banking

Banks are not institutions — they are a collection of contracts.

Key question: What problem exists? Which contract solves it?

  • Markets fail due to adverse selection and moral hazard.
  • Diamond–Dybvig: liquidity provision and bank-run risk stem from the same contract.
  • Loan contracts involve screening, monitoring, and incentive design.
  • Risk is redistributed within contracts.
  • Securitization and shadow banking involve repackaging risk.

Learning banking is learning contract theory. Everything else is detail.

Corporate Finance

Corporate finance is not about memorizing decisions, but about understanding incentive design.

  • Agency problems
  • Information asymmetry
  • Modigliani–Miller as benchmark
  • Debt as discipline

Focus: problem → mechanism

Econometrics

Econometrics is about understanding estimators.

  • β vs β̂ vs β̃
  • OLS as optimization
  • Gauss–Markov logic
  • Estimator behavior

Asset Pricing

Asset pricing is about pricing risk.

  • Mean-variance
  • CAPM
  • APT
  • Risk → return

Behavioral Finance

Behavioral finance is about systematic deviations.

  • Limits to arbitrage
  • Bias → behavior → price
  • Sentiment and attention
