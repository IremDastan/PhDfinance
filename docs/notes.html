PhD Qualifying Exam — Study Notes

How I Studied

My most important realization: learn derivation, not memorization. I studied each topic at three levels: intuition (why it's correct), mathematical derivation (how to derive it), and economic meaning (what it means).

The real test: close the paper and derive everything from scratch. If you can't, you haven't learned it.

Learning ≠ solving. What makes the difference isn't working hard, but working correctly.

1. Banking

Banks are not institutions — they are a collection of contracts.

Key question: What problem exists? Which contract solves it?

  • Markets fail due to adverse selection and moral hazard
  • Diamond-Dybvig model: liquidity provision and bank run risk stem from the same contract
  • Loan contracts = screening + monitoring + incentive design
  • Risk is redistributed within contracts
  • Securitization, shadow banking = repackaging risk, rewriting contracts

Learning banking is learning contract theory. Everything else is detail.

2. Corporate Finance

Corporate finance is incentive design under information asymmetry.

Key question: What is the incentive problem, and what mechanism solves it?

  • Modigliani-Miller: if no frictions, financial structure is irrelevant — understand why MM breaks down
  • Debt disciplines managers (Jensen-Meckling)
  • Myers-Majluf: financing order (internal > debt > equity) reflects information structure
  • IPOs, dividends, governance = same problem: information, incentives, control

Learning corporate finance is understanding the incentives and information structure behind financial decisions.

3. Econometrics

Econometrics is about understanding where estimators come from and what they measure.

Key distinction:

  • β — population parameter
  • β̂ — sample estimate
  • β̃ — alternative estimators under different assumptions
  • OLS derivation: minimization → normal equations → projection logic
  • Gauss-Markov: under what assumptions is β̂ BLUE? What does "best" mean?
  • Heteroskedasticity & serial correlation: how does distribution of β̂ change?

I used Greene (the green book) and derived every result from scratch.

4. Asset Pricing

Asset pricing = understanding how risk is priced.

Key question: Which risks are priced, and why do some yield a premium?

  • Markowitz (1952): mean-variance tradeoff → efficient frontier
  • CAPM (Sharpe 1964): priced risk is systematic risk (β), not total volatility
  • Derive CAPM: MV optimization → market portfolio → Security Market Line
  • Roll's Critique (1977): market portfolio is unobservable
  • APT: multiple systematic factors can be priced — a generalization of CAPM
  • Fama-French, Fama-MacBeth: multifactor empirical models

The logic is always the same: risk → pricing.

5. Behavioral Finance

Behavioral finance = systematic irrationality and its price impact.

Key framework: What bias → what behavior → what market outcome?

  • Shleifer-Vishny: irrational behavior persists when arbitrage is costly
  • Prospect theory: loss aversion + reference dependence + concave/convex value function
  • Heuristics: overconfidence, representativeness, availability
  • Market outcomes: momentum, overreaction, underreaction, disposition effect
  • Baker-Wurgler sentiment; attention-based models

Learning behavioral finance is understanding how mistakes systematically translate into price changes.
